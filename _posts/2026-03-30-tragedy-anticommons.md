I got Shrouded once. For privacy, I’ll keep the details vague. In short, I was discussing a Hack Club project, and my message got reported as advertising. I deleted my message and moved on, but it was surprising. I want to respect the intent of each channel. But…what is the intent of each channel? Who is doing the intending? And, if a channel needs to change, who gets to say so?

This is on my mind again because people have asked me to hold more debates, and I’m shopping for a venue. I’ve been using my personal channel, #eps-conduit. But the debates have the potential to bring a lot of life to wherever they are held, and I’ve [written before](https://news.hackclub.com/three-futures-slack/) about personal channels draining the life from public channels, so I have mixed feelings about keeping them in my own channel. But if not there, where else do I put them?

I don’t want to create a new channel for them. The Slack already has too many channels. It’s a big community, but even with 4500 monthly active users, I feel like we’re huddled around a trash barrel fire in the ruins of some enormous city. But I am reluctant to use any existing channel other than mine because I don’t want to step on anyone’s toes.

But that’s the problem: sometimes channels have so many stakeholders that in effect, nobody has the right to speak for the channel, or change the channel, even if change is needed.

When {% slack_user Rowan, U080A3QP42C %} [introduced Prometheus](https://news.hackclub.com/prometheus-distributed-care/), he talked about The Tragedy of the Commons. This applies to some channels. But others are subject to what Michael Heller called The [Tragedy of the Anticommons](https://repository.law.umich.edu/facarticles/609/), where competing claims of ownership make it so no one has a clear right of action.

The difference is this: in the Tragedy of the Commons, nobody fixes a problem because they all think it’s someone else’s responsibility to fix it. In the Tragedy of the Anticommons, nobody fixes a problem because they’re all scared that it’s somebody else’s right to fix it.

I ran into a similar problem with #confessions, where the community was divided 50/50 on how the channel should operate, so that any arrangement would leave half the users feeling dispossessed. The jury is still out on how those changes are working in terms of channel quality, but in terms of my public relations skills, it was a clear failure; in hindsight I should have talked to more people first.

With that lesson in mind, I saw an opportunity to solve the Tragedy of the Anticommons in #ysws. Thoughtful commenters raised the problem that the channel was getting spammy, but there was a clear divide. This time, I advised the community to poll the channel first, which revealed a clear preference towards keeping ads but limiting their frequency. Four community members stepped up to be channel owners: {% slack_user anson chung, U06P19MEXEZ %},  {% slack_user cookie with coffee, U0A2EKA9FAL %}, {% slack_user rohan, U0A17JG84G6 %}, and {% slack_user sahil, U08PUHSMW4V %}. They have come up with a system to balance the interests of advertisers and audience.

I’m very happy with this outcome. It tells me that with proper planning, we can tackle the Tragedy of the Anticommons (and the Tragedy of the Commons).
