I like analogies, so I think I will do another one. Consider the following situation: You want to throw a party, but you're broke. Your friend offers to fund the whole thing, food, venue, even a big speaker! But only if you hold it at his house, on his schedule, and only invite people he approves of. You could say no, hold no party, and stand on principle. Or you could say yes, throw a great party for the people who can make it, and use the experience to negotiate better terms next time. Neither choice is abhorrently wrong, but only one of them actually results in a party.

I've seen the [#meta](https://hackclub.slack.com/archives/C0188CY57PZ/p1764181534364829) [threads](https://hackclub.slack.com/archives/C0188CY57PZ/p1780688267307219), and it seems like season two of "region locked hackathons" is airing. Nobody likes being told "nuh uh, not for you," especially when what's being offered is pretty cool. But I think the conversation keeps missing the mark.

Where the money comes from matters more than you think. Hack Club has historically been pretty clean in this regard. Our donors were [private individuals and companies](https://v3.hackclub.com/philanthropy/supporters/), and they generally gave with no strings attached. That's extremely rare, and we were really lucky for this (this is what Christina does btw). Near the end of 2025, we entered our first major government-adjacent [partnership with AMD](https://www.amd.com/en/developer/resources/technical-articles/2025/amd-x-hack-club.html). AMD's sponsorship loosely traces back to President Trump's ["America's AI Action Plan,"](https://www.ai.gov/action-plan) a policy framework explicitly designed to channel government resources into private-sector AI infrastructure to give the U.S. an edge over Chinese AI. Lisa Su, AMD's CEO, subsequently [joined the President's Council of Advisors on Science and Technology](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-trump-announces-appointments-to-presidents-council-of-advisors-on-science-and-technology/) a few months later. We can tell that AMD is not just your average wafer company right now, but an arm of U.S. AI policy.

When you take money from a government initiative, you take the restrictions that come with it. There is no way the U.S. government will fund a hackathon for teenagers in Denmark unless [Trump gets Greenland in return](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland_crisis) (which a hackathon is unlikely to do).

This is the same reason the official NASA logo could not be placed next to a Hack Club logo with an X between them on the Stardance posters. Every piece of material must be cleared with [NASA's Office of Communications](https://www.nasa.gov/nasa-brand-center/merchandise-approvals/). The design would have been rejected for incorrectly displaying the Hack Club logo with the NASA logo, since it would imply that NASA directly endorses Hack Club. NASA is a government agency, and everything it touches is subject to scrutiny that would make #meta look like compliments. We have seen the consequences when Hack Club is not explicit about how relationships work, and [Laura Loomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laura_Loomer) (far-right political activist for the uninitiated) [tore Hack Club apart on Twitter](https://x.com/LauraLoomer/status/1898943141798924580), and [Elon Musk took notice](https://x.com/elonmusk/status/1898977274273161652). These organizations have to be strict because they have their own reputations to protect.

As I looked more into region locks, I realized that they are not a new thing either.

Think about how fulfillment works. Hack Club ships prizes all over the world all the time, but not everywhere, and not always the same prizes. Some items can't clear customs. Some countries have import restrictions. Some vendors won't fulfill outside certain regions. We already made our point that we are a US-based nonprofit, and we can largely guarantee first-class support in the US and Canada, since the infrastructure is there and it's so close. The [Eurozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurozone) is a close second in terms of reliability, but anything outside of that is no man's land. We would have to divert money and time that would go into events and prizes into boxes going halfway across the world, breaking import and electronics laws, and held ransom by governments with import taxes.

If we are causing a commotion about the U.S. government funding hackathons only for people in the U.S., where are the protests against the U.S.-based [Framework](https://frame.work/) not serving India, the [third-largest country of professional developers](https://www.jetbrains.com/lp/devecosystem-data-playground/#Top%2015%20countries%20by%20number%20of%20professional%20developers)?

## The fix is not "just make it global"

I want to bring up the easy counterargument in every thread like this: that Hack Club could have just rejected AMD, or [ace-attorney'd away](https://objection.lol/) the geo-lock, or funded the non-U.S. component separately. Perhaps! I can't rule out every possibility or read into futures that never happened. But the realistic outcome was not "same event, open to everyone." The realistic outcome was no event. And no event helps exactly zero people. There's a version of idealism that protects your principles at the cost of outcomes. Turning down a partnership because it doesn't serve everyone everywhere equally sounds noble and virtuous until you realize the people it would have helped are now just... not helped. The goal of Hack Club is not to help every single teenager in every single country, but to give as many teenagers as possible access to cool things. Sometimes you can do that globally. Sometimes you can only do it regionally. Both are better than flat-out nothing.

## What you can actually do about it

You might be asking: "well okay, you complained that offering the same thing to literally everyone is hard and you want to please people with money without giving me a real solution, the hell am I supposed to do with just that!?" and you would be right! Here comes the fun part: if you want more international opportunities, someone has to go find them. Someone said it in the thread, and they were right, lobby organizations in your country, find donors who care about your region, and build the thing you want to see. [Midnight](https://midnight.hackclub.com/) happened because people decided they wanted to go to Austria and made it happen. [Beest](https://beest.hackclub.com/) happened because Euan had a budget and a vision. [Macondo](https://macondo.hackclub.com/) happened because Nathan wanted a real event in South America. These events exist because of humans, not because a world leader launched an AI initiative.

If you are a non-U.S. Hack Clubber who is frustrated with these region-locked events, that frustration is valid and worth channeling at something. Just make sure you're pointing it at the right thing. The target is not HQ, but the reality that the largest pools of capital for teen tech programs with Hack Club are concentrated in the United States, and are influenced by United States policy, and flow through United States institutions. Changing that will likely take years, if not decades, to fully catch up with the states, but it is not impossible. Like I said in [my hiring article](https://news.hackclub.com/essays/how-hiring-works/), talk is cheap. Great ideas like <SlackChannel id="hib" /> never saw the light of day because we accepted the status quo of "old politician says we can't do it, therefore we can't do it." When have real hackers actually knelt before governments to accept the way things are? Hackers make the system work in their favor, and if you are a real hacker, you will help lead the efforts in your local area.

Not every door will be open to you 24/7. This is true at Hack Club and everywhere. Some items we can't ship to you. Some hackathons we cannot open to you. Some AMAs will not be during the day in your timezone. We will always try our best to give you the most opportunities as humanly possible, but that also means you may not be able to do every single one.
