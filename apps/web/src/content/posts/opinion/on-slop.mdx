I've reviewed software for a few programs recently, and it's never really been harder to do so. Programs are hit with, frankly, an absurd amount of submissions. I used to look at reviewers with disdain - come on, it takes like a few minutes for a review, and so how hard could it possibly be for them just to review my project waiting for a month? Just take a look at any support channel. There are literally hundreds of tickets looking for reviews in #horizons-help and #macondo-help. There are people who wait weeks or even a month in some circumstances, even as reviewers are actively reviewing.

Standing on the other side, it's not great. It's pretty demoralizing to watch the onslaught of slop pouring in, beginner projects, broken ones, occasionally fraud. And of course, most people aren't trying to make low-quality, broken, sloppy submissions. They often just want to get a given X reward, or visit Y event. Queues fill with hundreds in the backlog, a slog to clear at scale. 

Slop is a term we often toss around like candy. And yet, despite that, there exists no good definition of it. We can definitively classify some projects as slop - for example, Tic-Tac-Toe game #53 <Emoji name="ai-gradient" /> is probably something sloppy. These projects oftentimes serve no purpose or have no value. And with the growth of AI, the line between beginner projects, made with care but a lack of experience, and vibeslop (also often beginners) begins to blur to an extreme.

Before AI, the quality of a project, just the sheer weight of what it was, used to carry a useful signal about how much time was spent. You indeed couldn't build a functioning chat app all that easily (at least, not with a little bit of prompting to your LLM of choice). Just the fact that it existed was enough to justify the effort backing it - and of course, the commits to show progress. Now we deal with an onslaught of slop, where truly beginner projects are of a lower complexity than AI generated projects. This is no longer a useful signal in any shape or form. The time no longer indicates much as either - the number of times I've seen typed-out AI-generated code, all to inflate hours, is staggering. It's impossible to know if someone was "really" spending that much time thinking, making, developing, or whatnot. Save for a few incredibly high-quality or innovative projects, this is the vast majority of projects being reviewed right now. And as we have sloppy projects with AI, we have sloppy projects without. If you haven't yet, you should read Deven's Shitty Projects page in the introduction to the YSWS handbook. Everybody has a shitty first project, and most Hack Clubbers are new folks. We want to reward the time they spent building. In reality, the true distinction between sloppy and shitty ends up being the effort and process, which we can capture with a variety of signals, but is a nuance to be communicated.

In the face of all of this, I have a question. What exactly are we optimizing for? What do we care about? The Weighted Project model incentivizes programs to get teens spending more hours... doing what exactly? Learning? Building? Shipping? Because all of these hours are certainly not worth the same amount. There are people who build incredible things with AI, projects that I use on a daily basis that have more AI-written code than human-written code, and there are people who use AI as a means to an end, to get some predefined rewards, without going through the process of learning and understanding what they built (or just make HTML/CSS vibeslop en masse). The WP model is supposed to alleviate this issue by exerting itself as a program-level pressure, rather than at an individual level. It becomes no surprise, then, that when programs move this down to an individual level by directly providing them some $ worth of grants per hour they spend, that this transactional nature leads to more slop - which is a topic to talk more in depth about another time.

On the other side, the WP model also has the side effect of reviewslop. Internal justifications are supposed to prevent a reviewer from submitting approvals without checking projects, and at that they do a pretty good job (over the previous system of doing nothing). But justifications are plenty, and audits are few, and I've seen less than stellar justifications enter Unified, across multiple YSWS programs. It's also a very conflicting goal. When the bottleneck is review, and when the queue fills up with literally dozens of projects a day, sheer throughput becomes the primary goal, and quality goes second. Oftentimes I see people complaining that they got deducted just because of commits - and yeah, it's true we may rely too much on commits. Commit count is an easy cop-out for reviewers to trivially claim they've checked the "project quality" without actually checking the project itself. I mean, hey, it's a really easy signal, and it's easy to quantify with the brilliant approved = min(hours, commits) . Every step of the review process pushes reviewers towards being lazier, and review quality is entirely dependent on who leads the review team, and what expectations they set by example. None of these are bad metrics in and of themselves, but at scale collapse into very simple metrics (commit count) without checking what actually happened in those commits, if they're consistent, if they reflect work, to give the benefit of the doubt. And this, oftentimes, is the core issue of why "hire more reviewers" isn't the right solution.

Sometimes, people wonder why approval criteria are so strict, and why deflations exist for low commit counts. When reviewers look at projects, there is often barely anything to go by. Reviewers receive your Hackatime hour time total and your repository. There is nothing available for reviewers to see otherwise. I personally use a tool of my own to view when people spent time coding, but otherwise the actual information of your Hackatime heartbeats (filepaths, languages worked, etc.) is strictly restricted to fraud review (understandably). Fraud review itself is also generally more time-intensive.

So what can we do? Journaling certainly is not a one-size-fits-all solution, for a variety of reasons (perhaps to be elaborated upon in a new article?), and scale is a problem that certainly is not going to reduce in magnitude. And as much as I wish there were a simpler solution, the truth is nuanced. YSWSes should have clear review guidelines, and more automated/highly expedited systems to prevent obviously-not-approvable projects from reaching reviewers in the first place - things like incorrect demo links, nonexistent or AI-generated READMEs, etc, and make it clearer to submissions the actual requirements. I don't mean a doc to link to that technically explains things, I mean concrete, hard facts that tell people what to do and what not to do. The value gained from making things vague, or not telling people directly, offloads extra work onto reviewers to figure out what it means themselves (for example, arbitrary difficulty tier systems). Rewards, if possible, should not be tied to hours themselves, but at least incentivize good quality (for example, the Macondo starfruit). AI usage policy should be defined if possible, and no, limiting it to 30% is a lazy cop-out (which, tangentially, has always somewhat bothered me - 30% of what? time? code? effort?) and serves as a way to hide behind numbers. I've seen multiple different policies on this matter, and what I'm personally a fan of is the (perhaps arbitrary) slop rejection - direct and evident vibeslop. We should have a large, public collection of past reviews, to demonstrate by example to reviewers what standards we have. 

None of these solutions are perfect, or solve anything on their own, of course. But I hope that with as many small steps in the right direction as we can, we are able to handle these growing pains.
