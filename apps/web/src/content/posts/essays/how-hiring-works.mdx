Greetings Slacker News,

Consider the following situation: You are in class, and your teacher tells you to work in groups of 3. This is a big project that will be worth 10% of your final grade, so choose your people wisely! You got Jimmy and Cassy, two of your best friends, to the left of you. You also have John and some other kid whose name you forgot the name of to your right. Ignoring the <SlackChannel id="meta" /> threads for a second, who are you realistically picking? If you pick your friends, you work on the project, and since everyone is already on the same wavelength, you do great. If you pick John and the unknown, you are picking a gamble between a productive group and a total flop.

I have seen quite a lot of posts and comments about how Hack Club hires/chooses people to run programs. Some of the terms I see thrown around are "elitism" and "picking only their friends." I believe this is not a problem limited to Hack Club, but life in general. I am personally involved with HQ on some of their big programs, so I think I could offer some advice. I'm aware I might be the wrong person to say this is all fine, given that I did, in fact, end up inside the thing, but I don't want to see talented people discouraged from asking how to help just because they don't know anyone on the inside. After all, you have to start from somewhere!

This is a feature of human nature: taking risks can be scary, and I get that. From an outsider's point of view, it sucks! I was in the job market (eww employment) too, and seeing beginner jobs that require experience feels like such a rug pull!

But let's take the role of the people hiring for a second. If you are leading a team at Hack Club (or literally anywhere), you are looking for people who are super hyped up and excited about their work, but you also want people who are dependable and reliable. People who can really commit the time required to do the job and align with your vision for the thing. The problem is that this is naturally a small group of people. People who are hiring are likely to go with someone they have worked with before and had a good time with, rather than take the gamble on someone new.

Many months ago, I tried to hire a review team for one of the big YSWS events, and after hearing complaints about favoritism, I decided to let anyone and everyone join if they asked. I would try to personally hand-hold them through the process, get the setup in place, and help check their reviews as needed. It started out great, but quickly grew into a mess that required more people to wrangle and control. The first problem is that we had people join the team, do 10 or 20 reviews, and then just disappear into thin air. I could not depend on them to do their job, so I had to hire more people. The next problem was more silent, but the quality of reviews varied wildly from person to person. I could not trust that the same project would get the same decision if I asked two different people, so I had to spend more time on training. Finally, since everyone had different perceptions of what a "good" project was, more time was spent arguing over who was right than actually sending our reviews, so I had to be the final arbiter on every minor dispute.

Would all of this have been avoided by just hiring people I'd worked with before? Probably not fully, but enough of it that I get why people default to it. Cool things like delegation and documenting everything could help, but the majority of the decisions you make in your position come from experience, not from a Slack Canvas telling you how to think.

The good part is that the way in is easier than you might think. People do get added to teams, programs, and review roles all the damn time! Some of them were complete strangers a month ago! The issue is that nobody has ever written down where the door is. You can't ask Amp "how get on (insert program) team?" Wanna know what the path is really like from the inside? Someone does good work, someone notices, someone mentions them to someone else, and eventually they get asked. Sounds [shrimple](https://tenor.com/view/shrimp-as-that-clash-royale-hee-hee-hee-haw-gif-25054781)! Except the "someone notices" step kinda assumes you're already somewhat close to the people doing the noticing. If you're not active in the right channels, shipping things publicly, or asking the right people, you can be genuinely cracked and be invisible at the same time. This is not on purpose, and we don't have a group of people going "fuck this guy in particular." It's more that the system runs on visibility, and visibility requires knowing how to be visible, which requires knowing the system. (recursion anyone?)

The good news is that you can get around all that mess by just asking directly. The bad news is that you have to know who to ask, what to ask for, and how to present yourself, which brings me back to the part where I actually try to be helpful.

You might be asking: "well okay, you complained that hiring is hard and getting hired is hard without giving me a real solution, the hell am I supposed to do with just that!?" and you would be right! I don't make all the hiring decisions, so I can't give you the secret method that will get you into any position you want for free (legit + working 2026 + not scam), but here are a few quick pointers that should help you get the edge over others.

- **Respect the person's time.** Everyone around here is human, and we get annoyed when you bug us too much. It also does not help your chances of getting hired lol
- **Why are you better?** Applications are the only place where you can talk about yourself at length, and people won't call you egotistical, so take advantage of it. One of the questions I like to ask is "why should I choose you over other people?" This isn't just to reduce the process to a rat race, but to help me get a sense of where your head is at.
- **Start small.** Asking to help out with a smaller program is more likely to get a yes rather than starting with a huge program. This is not because we don't think you're good enough, but a smart person is not going to hand someone a truck if this is their first time driving; a [Corolla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toyota_Corolla) would make more sense.
- **Talk is cheap.** If you are asking to join a hardware team, it would be wise to show that you actually know what you're talking about. Anyone can answer hardware questions by outsourcing them to an AI model, but building a project that doesn't just follow a tutorial from the ground up takes some gusto. Bonus points if you are shipping stuff to the <SlackChannel id="ship" /> channel. Too many people are [all bark no bite](https://www.wordnik.com/words/all%20bark%20and%20no%20bite).
- **Be specific.** "I want to get more involved with Hack Club" tells me nothing. "I've been reviewing ships in <SlackChannel id="fallout-checkpoint" /> for a few weeks, and I'd love to be a proper reviewer for the next event," tells me you've already been doing the work and want to step it up. One of these is easier to say yes to.
- **Your actions follow you.** Hack Club is not that big. Saying that we talk about people behind their backs is not the right phrasing, but people often ask each other to see what their experience with X person was, so if you do mess up (hey we are all human), use it as a learning opportunity and not a chance to jump ship for a new role (you won't believe how many people try to do this)
- **Don't fucking use AI.** You are the one applying here, not an AI. If we wanted to hire an AI, we would have done so already. We are asking for humans because we want humans, not a robot. If you use AI, it shows you do not care, and therefore I should not care about your application.

Hopefully, these pointers will help in your adventures of getting involved with HQ. The skills you gain from Hack Club will help you in real life, not just the programming skills, but much more. I have personally learned how to work better with people, how to give and accept good feedback, how to formulate arguments more effectively, and how to be a better person overall, and I hope the same is true for you, too. The reality is that the world is built on connections. Both fiber optic and flesh + feelings. Learning to leverage it is what separates ordinary people from real rockstars.
