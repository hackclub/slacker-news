A month ago I started a new role at Hack Club called Quality and Integrity Manager, which sounds really fancy. What it actually means is that I spend my days reviewing other teenagers’ project submissions and deciding whether they are real submissions or low quality effort projects made just to get something in exchange.

I want to explain why anyone should care about that.


**What is even happening here**

Quick tour, in case you’re new.

Hack Club is a nonprofit for teenagers who like building things. The cool part, the thing that makes it different from basically every other “learn to code” org, is that you actually get to build the stuff you’ve always wanted to build instead of just completing a course. And then you get something real in exchange for shipping it: a Raspberry Pi, a domain, a flight to a hackathon.

A lot of that happens through YSWS, which stands for You Ship We Ship. Someone proposes a program: “build a Minecraft mod and I’ll send you server credits.” Teenagers ship projects against it. Reviewers look at the projects and decide whether they’re real, hours logged match the project, and there’s no fraud. Approved projects go into one shared database, weighted by the hours the maker actually put in. Program authors then get funded by Hack Club based on how many weighted projects their program produced.

So when I say “a weighted project,” I mean a unit in that shared database. One ship, with hours attached, that has cleared the bar.

This concept is very interesting, because it’s the closest thing Hack Club has to a common currency.


**The boring-sounding idea?**

If the weighted project is going to be a currency, it should mean the same thing no matter which program it came from.

One hour of shipped work in program A should reflect the same effort, the same standard, the same realness as one hour in program B. If that holds, then “weighted project” stops being an accounting line and becomes something much more useful: a stable unit you can build other things on top of.

I think about it like money. A dollar is a dollar whether you earned it at a job or found it on the sidewalk. You don’t have to inspect it before spending it. You just trust the unit, and that trust is the thing that lets the whole economy run on top of it.

Right now, that trust isn’t fully there. There’s a common pattern where program X has way more weighted hours than program Y, but if you actually open the submissions, program Y’s projects are so much cooler. The submitters clearly invested more time in their projects, the work is more ambitious, the ships are more interesting. Program X just had a looser review process. On paper, X looks like the runaway success and Y looks like it’s underperforming, when the truth is closer to the opposite.

When the unit is trustworthy, that goes away. You can launch a brand new program and know exactly what it’s producing. Compare a program that’s been around for two years against one that’s two weeks old, honestly. Look at the database and say “huh, this kind of program is taking off, what if we leaned in.”

And that matters because the version of Hack Club I want to exist is one where a teenager can look at a program, decide it sucks in some specific way, and go run and create the thing that replaces it. Where every year doesn’t look like the last one. That kind of constant reinvention only works if there’s a foundation underneath it that doesn’t wobble. You can’t experiment if your measuring stick changes length depending on who’s holding it.


**Everyone kinda knew, but nobody had it written down**

It’s not like people had no idea what a good project looked like. Everyone had a general sense. If you saw a well-built videogame with 25 hours of work behind it, you knew that was real. If you saw a half-finished website with 40 hours logged, you knew something was off.

The problem was the middle. The edge cases. A project that was decent but maybe not 20-hours-of-work decent. A submission that technically worked but felt like it was thrown together in an afternoon. For those, the answer depended on which person you asked. One might say approve it, another might say reject it, and both of them would have a reasonable explanation for their call. The rules existed as a kind of shared intuition, passed around in conversations and Slack dms, and if you happened to catch the right person on the right day you’d get one answer, and if you caught someone else you’d get a different one.

That works fine when you have a handful of programs and a small team. It stops working when you have dozens of programs, hundreds of reviewers, and thousands of submissions flowing into one shared database.

So we wrote the rules down. They live at [hackclub.com/ysws-rules](https://https://hack.club/ysws-rules). Every ship that lands in the database has to clear them. The point isn’t to be prescriptive about what teenagers can build (build whatever you want, that’s the whole game), it’s to take those judgment calls that used to depend on who you asked and give them one consistent answer.

For the past few weeks I’ve been going program by program, flagging issues to authors directly and gathering feedback as we iterate.

But writing down the rules only solves half the problem. The other half is: what happens when they don’t get followed?


**The funny part**

Imagine you’re a reviewer and a borderline project comes across your desk. It’s not great, but it’s not obviously bad either. You could reject it and move on. But if you approve it, your program gets credited for those hours, which means more funding.

Nobody sits there thinking “I’m going to approve bad projects on purpose.” That’s not how it works. What happens is subtler: when approving is free and rejecting has a cost, the thumb is always on the scale. Across hundreds of submissions, that tiny pressure adds up. Programs that are strict with their reviews end up with fewer hours than programs that aren’t, and the strict programs look like they’re underperforming. The incentive structure quietly rewards the wrong behavior, even when every individual reviewer means well.

Fines exist to fix that math.

When I pull a random sample from the database and find hours that shouldn’t be there, those hours get removed. The program that approved them gets fined $12 per removed hour. They can earn $8.50 of that back by properly re-processing the project. The remaining $3.50 is a permanent fine. Fine money goes into an HCB fines org.

The point isn’t to punish anyone for missing things. Every reviewer misses things. I miss things. A single bad approval isn’t a sign of a bad program. But fines make it so that doing the review properly is the cheaper option.


**What I actually want**

If we get this right, almost nobody should ever have to think about it. The database just works. A weighted project means a weighted project. Reviewers do their thing, programs run their thing, and the foundation holds.

And then the interesting work, the part where some 16-year-old decides that the way Hack Club runs programs is broken and goes off to build a better version, or proposes a program for something nobody’s tried yet, gets to happen on top of something solid, and we have a clear indicator of whether it succeeded or not.

That’s the job.
