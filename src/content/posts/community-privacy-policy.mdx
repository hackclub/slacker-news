Our [Terms and Privacy Policy](https://hackclub.com/privacy-and-terms/) govern how we use your data. But what governs how *everyone else* uses it?

Slack users have each other’s messages, pictures, computer activity history, contact information, and more. This leads to regular controversy, especially over sharing messages outside of the Slack, and giving AIs access to the Slack. Typically, one side argues that their privacy was violated, and the other side argues that (1) the Slack is not a private space and (2) there are no rules against what was done.

As often happens, the *real* disagreement is beneath the surface, in unspoken values. There is a spectrum of attitudes in play:
You have no right to privacy on the Slack. If you don’t want something getting out, don’t say it.
You have some right to privacy on the Slack, but these uses don’t violate it.
You have a large right to privacy on the Slack, and these uses violate it.

So long as the Slack has no agreement on which of these views prevails, conflict is inevitable,when it happens, nobody will be satisfied by the other side’s arguments because they don’t touch the underlying values.

In short, you need to agree on how much privacy you give each other.

This is doubly true because the Fire Department has limited power to enforce rules here. When people ask the FD’s permission they can say no, but there is little to be done about people who don’t ask and simply do. But norms can go where rules fear to tread.

So we’re going to build these norms together as a community. The first step is to have a live, in-person discussion. **It’s not a debate** because the issue is too complicated to be reduced into two opposing sides. Debating will occur, but the goal is different:


We will put together several proposed community privacy policies
We will put together survey instruments to see how people feel about them

The Forum will be held on Saturday, April 4, at 11 AM ET in a huddle in <SlackChannel id="community" />.

This will be followed by an asynchronous phase where we survey the larger community about each of the proposals. If there is one clear winner, we’ll adopt it. If there isn’t, we’ll iterate and try again. An adopted community privacy policy will have the FD’s backing, but it will really gain its force through majority support in the community.
