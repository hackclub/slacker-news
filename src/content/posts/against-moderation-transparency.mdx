Moderation transparency was introduced to me as a conflict between the Fire Department and everyone else. But I’ve finally realized that this is really a conflict between the community and itself. The FD is simply caught in the middle.

I try to maintain your good will: avoid things that are unpopular, do things that are popular. Increasing moderation transparency is thus very tempting for me: it would be instantly popular. But I find myself stuck on the unpopular side because my conscience won’t let me take the popular side. What I want to argue to you today is that moderation transparency is actually unethical.

By “moderation transparency” I mean any of the following:
- Logging more penalties (channelbans, MCG demotions, and warnings)
- Giving basic reasons for more penalties (as in “this is for spam”)
- Giving detailed reasons for more penalties (as in “here is a screenshot of the offending message”)

**Public Shaming**

I’ve seen enough community logs that when I click the button to make one, I know I'm making an open invitation to gossip, ridicule, and pass judgement on whomever the log is about. Every time I click that button I have an acute sense that I am harming one of the teenagers I’m supposed to protect.

I have seen some heartbreaking bans. People who cared about Hack Club, acted as public examples of what a good Hack Clubber should do, *and did very bad things that made it impossible to trust them in the community anymore*. This is the thing, people are complicated, both can be true at once. They did not deserve the added humiliation of having their worst decisions made public–and made into their legacy, because of course people remember how you leave. Or, if you come back, everyone knows what you did. It’s the digital equivalent of a room going silent when you walk in.

Hack Clubbers are normally very good about privacy, so it’s jarring to see your privacy so devalued here. But separately, public shaming is inimical to the mission of helping people grow and learn from their mistakes.

[Research indicates](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1996-01736-011) that shame and guilt are different emotions with different effects. Guilt is focused on a specific act and tends to cause growth and change. Shame targets a person’s whole self, is marked by a feeling of being exposed in front of a real or imagined audience, and tends to cause hostility and shifting blame to others.

To learn from a mistake, you have to accept you made one. The harsher the criticism, the harder it is to accept. “You are a good person, but you showed bad judgement” is a line people will digest. Slowly, grudgingly, but they will. “You are a bad person which is why you did a bad thing” is much harder. Public humiliation puts people on the defensive by making them feel like they are being judged to be a bad person. Doubly so if they know the community is saying so outright. This encourages people to blame everyone else instead of looking within. 

**What Happens on Slack does not Stay on Slack**

Separate from causing public humiliation within Hack Club, moderation transparency also risks lasting harm that follows banned users beyond Hack Club.

Things circulate on discord servers and signal groups. I know of two different cases of things being aired on other websites, and in one of those, people had their Slack messages screenshotted without permission. 

I’m also going to tell you that people talk. Before Hack Club, I worked in a different community of teenagers: high school debate. In that world, rumors regularly reached me about people from other schools, sometimes people I had never met. If someone gets banned for something like fraud or unwelcome advances, that is the kind of thing that could follow them and hurt them later in life. People deserve to leave their teenage mistakes behind, including serious ones.

**Transparency Hurts the Victims of Misconduct**

We had a case where a Hack Clubber was being harassed. We banned the harasser, who kept making alt accounts to harass them more. This was logged. Most people were horrified by the harassment, which is good, but a few were entertained and decided to make alt accounts of their own, harassing the same victim while posing as the original harasser. That was transparency in action.

In another case, there was a community log with the offender and the reason. From this, people immediately deduced who reported the offender, effectively breaking the anonymity that Shroud reports are supposed to have. Again: this is bad.

Most Hack Clubbers would never try to do this. You, dear reader, wouldn’t ~~steal a car~~ do this. But it only takes one.

**II. Common Defenses of Moderation Transparency**

**Does Transparency Prevent Abuse?**

I have heard that transparency ensures the FD does not play favorites and abuse their powers.

This argument fails because you do not know whether the FD is abusing their power unless you know the full details of the case. What looks like bias may just be the result of taking confidential information into account.

I have seen several cases where someone’s penalty was condemned as too harsh, but the critics were unaware of the full scope of wrongdoing. But giving everyone that level of insight is not transparency, it is a *surveillance state*. Transparency and Privacy are always in tension, and here is an instance where ignoring that tension in pursuit of transparency leads to the trampling of privacy.

**Does Transparency Reduce Speculation?**

This is the main argument I hear: if people don’t know, they will speculate. The truth is just the opposite: transparency causes speculation. Without community logs, people speculated about whether someone was banned. Then the log was added, and people speculated about why they were banned. Then the FD started saying which rule was broken, and now people speculate about what was done to break the rule.

This argument is chasing a mirage. The imagined future in which transparency prevents speculation will never come. By publicly announcing punishments, <SlackChannel id="community-logs" /> helps ensure that the gossip mill is as big as possible, and by giving reasons for punishments, it ensures that speculators have more material with which to speculate.

This isn't working. Right now we have gossip, abuse, and mistrust of moderation. The worst of everything.

Transparency causes speculation. I understand there are exceptions (as with a recent YSWS), but moderators make exceptions for exceptional cases. Transparency is defensible as a tool, not as a rule.

**Should the FD disclose when the subject requests it?**

This is the most reasonable case-by-case transparency proposal that I’ve seen. I don’t balk at it the way I do at more aggressive proposals. But I still don’t like it.

I think the underlying problem is that the Slack loves gossip. This is pretty common in teenage spaces (but not inevitable!). I think that what will actually happen is anyone who declines to share will be criticized for not sharing. All the heat that the FD gets will go to them instead. “If you have nothing to hide, you have nothing to fear”, people will say.

The community has opinions about how bad different rule violations are. People whose violations would be easily forgiven will ask for disclosure. People whose violations would hurt their reputation will not ask for disclosure. So everyone who doesn’t ask for disclosure will be presumed guilty of something really bad.

There is a better world that this, where everyone who is penalized has face-saving plausible deniability about what happened, and where the community just agrees to say:

**III. Actually, I am Not the Only One Who Can Prevent Forest Fires**

In these discussions, I’m always struck by the way that gossip is spoken of like an unstoppable law of nature. It isn’t. It’s a personal choice.

In dry leaves, fire catches and spreads. In wet leaves, it doesn’t.

Not gossiping is extremely easy; you just do nothing. If you want to make an effort, you could respond to gossip and speculation that reaches you with, "If it were me, I would hate having everyone talking about me like this.” And it doesn’t have to be every Hack Clubber doing this. It just takes enough to make the ground damp. People’s lives would get meaningfully better. You could start doing it today.

So will you? And if not, why not?
