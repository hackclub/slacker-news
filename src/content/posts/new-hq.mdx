Last week, [@indy](https://hackclub.enterprise.slack.com/team/UDK5M9Y13), [@msw](https://hackclub.enterprise.slack.com/team/U09UQ385LSG), and [@eps](https://hackclub.enterprise.slack.com/team/U09Q8MLTE58) showed up in my kitchen by surprise. “Hack Club is where we make things together,” Chris explained. “Where is Hack Club? If you are making things, then that is where Hack Club is.”

I started making Hack Club when I was 15, so back then Hack Club was in my bedroom in my parents’ house. But that year, I moved to the SF bay area to become a programmer. I lived in a group house for hackers. Max slept on the floor of my room, on a mattress pad he found on the sidewalk. Chris was living there too; it’s how we met.

It was an exciting time, but it also showed me something I didn’t like: There were institutions for hackers, and institutions for teenagers, but no institution for teenage hackers.

There are two support systems that should have done it: the education system, and the tech industry’s ecosystem of entrepreneurs. But both had other priorities. The education system is good at structured learning. But hacking is about unstructured experimentation. And hacking is about failing, again and again, until you find what works.

The logical thing is to found a new institution that does it differently. But the other support system–the tech industry’s entrepreneurial ecosystem–isn’t set up for this. If your goal is to make money, there are incubators and VCs and networking events and fellowships and a bottomless well of potential partners. But if you want to start a nonprofit, you’re swimming against an undertow. Every conversation was “What’s my equity upside?” And I would say “There isn’t one. I can pay you one-fifth of what your other pitches are offering.” I spent six months getting a single $50,000 donation, and I was so proud. That night, my roommate told me he secured a $500,000 investment for his startup from a 20-minute conversation.

I did find one person though. Through the San Francisco nonprofit network, I met someone who was running a worldwide network of investigative journalists. At a time when major newspapers were shuttering their foreign bureaus and cutting back on investigative reporting to save money, her reporters were publishing stories no one else would touch, from the [Iraqi government prosecuting the widows of alleged ISIS fighters](https://www.marieclaire.com/politics/a23571895/wives-of-isis/), to the [extraordinary challenges for women serving in the then-NATO-backed Afghan military](https://www.nytimes.com/2018/10/05/magazine/afghanistan-women-security-forces.html). That was Christina.

Christina was the smartest person in the room, and she thought about business with a level of sophistication and ambition that Hack Club desperately needed. She agreed to become a board member. Later, she joined Hack Club full-time as co-founder.

Along the way, it became clear that the Bay Area was not the right place for Hack Club. Our lease was up anyway, so we decided to go stay with Chris’s family in Maine. That was March 2020.

Everything was already shutting down for COVID, but there was one last week where some places were still open. Chris went to an army surplus store and bought a bunch of props: camouflage nets, ammunition boxes, that sort of thing. For 10 nights in a row, we streamed the COVID Command Center. Millions of teenagers were sealed away inside their homes with nothing to do, and some of them decided to take a look at Hack Club.

We couldn’t stay with family forever. Christina showed us that Vermont, where she lived, shares a lot of California values. The mountains of Vermont are just like my home state: filled with weirdos living out some version of their dream life. Authentic, idealistic, and unapologetic. But Vermont has far more possibility than California. This year we’ll give 40 full-time fellowships to teenagers. In California, we would have succumbed to the financial and cultural pressure, and become just like everything else.

Christina started an AMA series, and one after the other got some of the biggest names in the tech world to show up to our tiny digital doorstep. We stayed at a string of rental properties. First, we lived in a little house in Charlotte. That’s where we did our Elon AMA. Next, we moved to the Mt. Philo Inn. Finally, we rented out a huge house on an island in Lake Champlain. By that point, it was the only rental property big enough to hold all of Hack Club. 

Up to that moment, we had put in six years of work and grown Hack Club to 2,000 members. In one month, we grew it to 10,000. After years of trying different things, we finally found the thing that worked.

In October 2020, we found the building you all know as Hack Club HQ. As Hack Club grew, the space grew with it. Each wave of gap years left their mark. HQ feels lived in, and well-loved. That makes it really special to me, and I think everyone who visits it can tell these things instantly.

We kept trying new things. We radically reinvented our events process. Originally, events ran on a traditional model: assign a budget, give periodic updates. Programs would sometimes take most of a year to launch, then get less than 20 Hack Clubbers. YSWS was an aggressive overthrow of the old system. We replaced traditional oversight with blue-sky freedom, and planned budgets with evolving budgets. This is the weighted project system that you all know, and while it has its flaws, it is better than anything that came before it, and it allowed Hack Club to do even more. Before, we had thousands of teenagers watching. Now we have tens of thousands of teenagers actually shipping projects, and several times that number watching and waiting to feel inspired.

As you joined us, Hack Club kept growing. We outgrew HQ and added Phil in 2022, then Tracy in 2023.

The thing I started building in my parents’ bedroom 12 years ago is real now. There is an institution by the teenagers, for the teenagers. But we aren’t done. There are so many more teenagers in the world who need Hack Club and don’t have it yet. We are going to reach all of them, and you are going to build things together that make Hack Club better than it has ever been.

HQ is special to everyone who has ever been there. We will always treasure it, and we will miss it. But we are only at the beginning of Hack Club’s journey, and Shelburne is running out of rental properties.

Hack Club is where we make things together. Where is Hack Club? Starting next month, Hack Club is at [212 Battery St, Burlington, Vermont](https://maps.app.goo.gl/Z2hpLYkChsgh4Kwp6). But Hack Club is also in your bedroom in your parents’ house, or your classroom at school, or wherever you are making things. Wherever we go, as long as we keep making things, we will always be together and we will always be Hack Club.
