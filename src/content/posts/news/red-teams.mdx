<SlackMention name="Felix Gao" id="U07BU2HS17Z" /> is organizing a red-teaming exercise for the Fraud Squad. We are looking for community members to apply. All applicants must be approved by Felix and <SlackMention name="eps" id="U09Q8MLTE58" />.

**What we are doing**

- People chosen as members of the red team will be given fake verified identities by the IDV team.
- Red teamers will submit three kinds of projects to YSWS programs:
  - Legitimate projects made normally
  - Legitimate projects made under unusual conditions
  - Fraudulent projects
- The Fraud Squad will encounter these projects while doing normal reviews. They will not know whether a project is the work of a red team member or a regular participant. They will investigate these like any other project.
- We will look at the results to measure and hopefully improve the Fraud Squad's accuracy.

**Why we are doing it**

The Fraud Squad has a hard job.

Some fraud cases are clear-cut. But others lack definitive proof one way or the other. There are unverifiable excuses; some people might be breaking rules accidentally. It's hard to be sure.

But fraud is too common to just give people the benefit of the doubt. Flavortown had 1335 unique shippers, but 324 cooked fraudsters, meaning that roughly 24% of people who made it to the submission stage were trying to commit fraud. Twenty-four percent! The numbers aren't perfect, but they make it clear that fraudsters will rob a YSWS blind if we let them.

And we can't take people at their word, either. I have seen people swear up and down that they are innocent, post passionate screeds against the Fraud Squad all over the Slack, try to go over the Fraud Squad's head and protest to adults at HQ, and then, when I look into their case, the proof of their guilt is so obvious I can literally watch a minute-by-minute replay of the fraud they committed. Some fraudsters confess. Others are shameless liars.

But at the same time, false positives do happen. They seem rare, but right now it's hard to tell.

This tradeoff between false positives and false negatives has no easy answer. We probably can't eliminate either. But we can reduce both, and the first step is producing verified examples so we can see what went wrong.
