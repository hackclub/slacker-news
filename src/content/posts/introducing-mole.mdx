If you strip away all the programs, the Slack, the memes, and the code, Hack Club has always been about one thing: Making sure that the youth of today have the tools and knowledge to build on the frontier of what's possible.

For years, that frontier was programming - suddenly, anyone, from a kid in San Francisco to a mother in Japan, could build the next billion-dollar startup from their house with just a laptop, an internet connection, and some free time. The computers allowed anyone to express themselves freely, interact with people from all over the world, and build real things with just words, symbols, and numbers. Hack Club was built for this world, to ensure that our future leaders deeply understood these systems, and knew how to hack on them to propel us all forward.

But in the past few years, the frontier has been gradually shifting. The one-person startup can now rival teams of hundreds. Programming has become less about the code and more about what the code does. Knowing how to code isn't as important as it was 10 years ago. That craft is being replaced by things like marketing, ideation, and management.

Hack Club is trying to prepare you for a world that is disappearing.

So we lie at a crossroads - either stick with what we know works well, keep pulling weighted projects, and pretend like nothing is changing, or press ahead into the unknown.

Hack Club is doing better than ever. We’re improving the lives of countless students across the globe, thousands of amazing projects have been shipped via our programs, and our Slack is more vibrant and active than it's ever been. Sticking with the status quo is tempting.

But what does that buy us? A local maximum. And while we sit happily on our little pile of ships and LOCs, we'll watch the frontier slowly drift away, further away from the programming languages of 2024 and towards craft and soul, and always wonder what we're missing out on. And the teens we pledged to serve would be left behind, dropped into a world that they don't understand.

Over the past few weeks, we've been having meetings with our donor network. After sharing metrics on AI use in Hack Clubber projects, all were worried, and we're inclined to agree. If the frontier, the cutting edge, changes, we need to push Hack Clubbers towards it, even if that means making drastic changes to the programs and systems that have worked so well for so long.

We're choosing to press ahead.

We're evolving Hack Club for the new frontier of AI, embracing new kinds of software like agents and assistants and exploring the immense potential of this technology. You'll see many changes - some subtle, some not so much - as we work to redefine our organization for this new landscape.

We know this decision might be unpopular, and we understand, but we want to be clear: AI is not going away, and we'd much rather foster knowledge and curiosity about this new tech rather than pretend it will.

This is uncharted territory, so we're going to make some mistakes. Some of our experiments might end up being great, others may end up horrible. Just like when Hack Club was founded all those years ago, we're venturing out into the unknown fog once again. Please bear with us - and help out if you can! - as we figure out what works and what doesn't.

We invite you, dear hacker, to follow along, and join us as we explore - and build - this new frontier.

With love and gratitude,
Hack Club HQ

(P.S. We're starting with the community. We believe AI tools are a great fit here, due to the conversational capabilities of LLMs and their impartiality. You may have already noticed our powerful new automated moderation tools at work in the Slack. We're also working on an [exciting experiment we're calling Mole](https://hackclub.slack.com/archives/C0266FRGT/p1775059206361919) - a new kind of community member. They should drop in early access later today - see you then.)
