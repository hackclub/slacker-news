Last week, I posted in <SlackChannel id="meta" /> about how Hack Club recognises contributors when they leave. The response surprised me. The post got 74 upvotes and no downvotes (quite rare in <SlackChannel id="meta" />), which by any measure is a pretty strong signal that this resonates with people. You can read the full thread [here](https://hackclub.slack.com/archives/C0188CY57PZ/p1775278905544579).

The conversation that followed was genuinely good. People pointed out that there are pages like [hackclub.com/team](hackclub.com/team) and [hackclub.com/acknowledged](hackclub.com/acknowledged) which exist. Someone suggested a former team page. Someone mentioned that the contributors page on GitHub recognises you. Peter made a fair point that the real privilege of Hack Club isn’t the credit, it’s the community itself, the people you meet, the 3am conversations, and that shared passion. I think that’s true.

However, I also think these two things can go hand-in-hand. You can love what you build here, what you learned here and still want someone to say goodbye properly when you go. That’s honestly how I feel. I like to think that my work done here was impactful but it was never really acknowledged enough by the people who really “managed” my work, which really made my work unseen.

What I was pointing at isn’t really about college applications or getting your name on a website. It’s about something smaller and something more personal. A cupcake. A sentence. A card. Something that says: we saw what you did here, it mattered, and we will miss you. Not a system, not a page, just a moment. That’s exactly what Hack Club was built on and stands for.

The people I’m talking about are the ones who ran events, brought in and mentored new members (including club leaders!), or just showed up consistently for a very long time, sometimes unpaid, and then left quietly. Hack Club is forward-looking by nature, which is normally a strength, but it means people will slip out the door without acknowledgement, and that’s a gap worth closing.

So why doesn’t this happen already? A couple theories: HQ is genuinely stretched thin, and a personalised farewell takes time that’s hard to find when you’re already drowning in DMs. Departures also often go unannounced, people quietly stop showing up, so there’s no natural trigger. And maybe the hardest one: Hack Club’s culture is so focused on what’s being built right now that looking back feels counterintuitive.

I don’t have a definitive fix, but the bar doesn’t need to be that high. A lightweight channel where departures get surfaced. A norm where team leads send a short personal note. A workflow that catches when someone goes quiet. The 74-0 suggests I’m not alone in thinking this matters.

Thoughts?
